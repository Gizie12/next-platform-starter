The Oromo-led protests that unfolded from 2014 to 2018 did not merely challenge a specific masterplan or localised land-grabbing: they destabilised the wider architecture of party-state domination in Ethiopia. Yet, the transition they precipitated did not yield a robust democratic opening. Instead, it can be read as a paradigmatic example of a democratic mask regime, in which reformist rhetoric, legal revisions, and carefully staged elections worked together to re-inscribe executive control.
